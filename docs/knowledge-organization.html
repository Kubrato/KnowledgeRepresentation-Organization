Knowledge Organization

This page shows how the project moves from an idea to a structure. There are two steps: the theoretical model (a mind map of items and relations) and the conceptual model (an ontology of classes and properties).

Theoretical model

The theoretical model was built starting from the text the project is based on. From this text the central subject and ten related items were identified, and the relations connecting them to Atatürk were annotated explicitly in the TEI encoding through a <listRelation> element. Further information describing each item was then recorded in its own CSV table as natural-language triple statements in a simple subject – predicate – object form, for example anitkabir – has construction date – 1953.
Read together, the items and their relations form a mind map: Atatürk sits in the centre and the items branch out from him through labelled relations, such as a mausoleum that is dedicated to him or a book he wrote.

From one model to the other

The two models describe the same project at different levels. The mind map answers "which items are connected?" The ontology answers "what kinds of things are these, and how are they linked in general?" The data on the next page must match the ontology exactly: every class and property in the model is used in the data, and nothing is used that is not in the model.

Conceptual model

Starting from the predicates and entities described in natural language in the theoretical model, each triple statement was mapped into RDF syntax. Here the individual items disappear and only their classes and properties remain: each class appears once, and arrows go from class to class.
Rather than inventing new terms, and rather than leaning on just one or two sources, the model deliberately reuses a range of existing, widely adopted vocabularies, each for what it describes best:

  • Schema.org (schema:), the main source for general types such as Movie, City, Event and Country.
  • CIDOC-CRM (crm:) for cultural-heritage-specific classes and properties: sites, people, physical dimensions and reference relations.
  • Dublin Core Terms (dct:) for dates, language, subject, creator and place (dct:subject, dct:spatial, dct:issued…).
  • FOAF (foaf:) for people (foaf:Person, alongside CIDOC-CRM) and for authorship and depiction (foaf:maker, foaf:depicts).
  • SKOS (skos:) for the abstract concept Kemalism and its relations (skos:related).
  • FRBR (frbr:) to model the book Nutuk at its four levels — Work, Expression, Manifestation and Item.
  • OWL (owl:) to link every entity to its DBpedia twin with owl:sameAs, connecting the dataset to the wider web.

Literals were made explicit by declaring their datatype with the XML Schema Definition (xsd:) language, and every item is linked to its Wikidata authority record so that the local data reconciles with an external, shared identifier. Where no suitable published class existed (for the bridge entities that hold the network together, such as the Six Arrows emblem, the Turkish language, the national assembly and the central bank), the closest CIDOC-CRM class was chosen (crm:E36 Visual Item, crm:E56 Language, crm:E74 Group).
